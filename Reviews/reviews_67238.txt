DPS has recently become quite popular on tiktok which caused me to hyperfixate on it once more, even though I never enjoyed it, I had been meaning to finish the book just for the sake of finishing it, so I used this opportunity to finish it today. In case you don't know, this book is based on the movie, and it was published 9 years after the movie was released. This movie didn't need to be a book. Perhaps it could've been a good book, if it had been written from Neil's or Todd's perspective, or if it had actually gone into depth about the movie's themes. Instead of doing any of those things, this book is simply the movie and its deleted scenes adapted into a book format. Movies tend to be fast-paced considering they usually only last around two hours, because of this they usually don't get to go in depth and detail about things in the same way a book can. This is where the problem comes in if you're going to adapt a movie into a book and not add anything, you end up with a book that comes off as rushed and shallow. Not only was barely anything added but some things were cut even. The scene where Neil and Todd chuck the desk set is a beautiful scene and one of my favorites, but in the book, it's cut short for no apparent reason. 3984902945	Contemporary general fiction, written and set in my own lifetime, hasn't formed a large part of my reading over the decades; I've tended to be more drawn either to older classics or to "genre fiction." But I've appreciated much of what I have read in this vein, and recognize its value in a healthy and well-balanced literary diet. So I'm now trying to be more intentional in seeking it out more often; and since I was looking for a quick read late last month, and this particular book has only 166 pages, it struck me as a natural choice. It happens to be a novelization of the 1989 movie of the same name (https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0097165/ ); but since I've never seen the latter, I can't compare the two, and approached it strictly as a novel on its own terms. (As I've commented in other reviews, I've never subscribed to the snob clerisy dogma that movie novelizations are inherently trash.)Our setting here is fictional 100-year-old Welton Academy, an elite private combination junior high/high school for boys located in a small Vermont town, in the fall and early winter of 1959. Born in 1948, author Nancy Horowitz Kleinbaum (who, sadly, died last year), a professional writer who did a number of movie novelizations in her career, was 11 years old at that time; but it's not her own lived experience that underlies the story. That experience would actually be that of movie scriptwriter Tom Schulman, who won an Academy Award for the movie screenplay this novel is based on, and who in turn drew for material on his own memories of attending a similar prep school in Nashville; one of his teachers there inspired the character of charismatic, maverick new English instructor Mr. Keating. (Schulman, however, was actually slightly younger than Kleinbaum; I'm guessing that he pushed the chronological setting back to a few years before his own high school experience in order to avoid complicating the plot with late 60s issues.) Rather than focusing on a single protagonist, we follow the shared experiences of a small group of 11th-grade boys, who will soon (with Keating's tacit encouragement) revive the sub rosa Dead Poets Society that he belonged to in his own student days at Welton.For me, this book was a window into a milieu totally outside my own experience: the world of the monied WASP elite and their pricey hot-house private school seedbeds for Ivy League enrollees. It's not an attractive milieu (and not meant to be). All of these boys have parents willing to ship them off to a boarding school just because it purports to be a ticket to a prestigious college; none of the parent-child relationships we're shown are at all healthy, and none of these parents have imparted any particular moral or spiritual foundation to their offspring. Welton purports to base itself on the "four pillars" of "Tradition! Honor! Discipline! Excellence!" but "defines" them with empty cliches, and in practice doesn't inculcate any particular character qualities except conformity, self-protection, and obedience to power, backed up by a regime of corporal punishment that Dickens' Mr. Squeers might have applauded. What passes for academic rigor consists of dishing out copious amounts of reading homework (but not necessarily helping the students understand what they're reading). Parental and academic authority here consistently comes across as an uncaring, self-serving and in some cases sadistic caricature, that's drawn with unrealistic one-sidedness.In contrast, Mr. Keating is presented as a representative of everything caring, warm and fuzzy, free and spontaneous, which the reader is meant (and manipulated) to identify with. To some extent, this sympathy is earned; the moral crisis at the climax of the book, where the question presented to the characters (and the reader) is whether to cravenly go along with the scapegoating of an innocent or to take a stand against it, is in line with the classic moral tradition of world literature. And the idea that assessing the greatness of a given poem is a "relatively simple" exercise that can be reduced and graphed as a mathematical formula is rightly called out as ludicrous. (I didn't think anybody would seriously claim that, but though the textbook and its author here are fictitious, the passage that's read is paraphrased from an actual influential textbook.) At the same time, not every idea that Keating propounds is worthy of endorsement either. He's certainly right to encourage his pupils to really live, to think for themselves and to be all they can be, and to appreciate great literature; but engaging with the latter is more than a matter of reading into it anything you want, and while disagreeing with a textbook author is fine, mutilating a book by tearing out passages you disagree with, throwing them in the trash and stamping on them is NOT fine. (At times, he appears to simply be getting the boys to substitute one form of group-think for another.)While the book has some profanity and vulgarisms (but no obscenity), that element isn't pronounced, there's no explicit or even implied sex, and interpersonal violence is confined to non-lethal beatings. But that said, for sensitive readers this novel could come with trigger warnings galore, including for teenage drinking.
