Brief Summary:Alice Law and Peter Murdoch, both Cambridge graduate students in the most challenging field of magical studies, transport themselves to Hell in order to rescue their deceased academic advisor, Professor Grimes. This particular professor is one of the most respected scholars in his field, and without him, both students feel that all of their academic toiling will have been wasted. So despite the steep price, they magic themselves into Hell. The majority of the novel details the various realms of Hell through which Alice and Peter must travel. Extended Metaphor, Wit & Humor:Surely by now, you are wondering if this book is an updated version of Dante. Nope. Of course Dante's works are referenced, but this novel is essentially a conceit--one giant allegorical metaphor--and it absolutely oozes the most delicious wit. When I first started, I had no idea that I was in for such fantastic hilarity. I hadn't read anything about the humor here at all. But the puns begin immediately, and it quickly becomes obvious that Kuang is making fun of everything we take too seriously. If as a student this ever applied to you, if you endured countless sleepless nights in service to your grade point average, if you poured over practice sites to obtain perfect standardized test scores and revised obsessively to win academic awards and scholarships; then this novel is your chance for catharsis...your chance to laugh. In Katabasis, Kuang takes on academia, the western canon, and even America. I laughed out loud at least once every three pages, and the moment I hit the last page, I flipped right back to the beginning for a reread. Style & Structure:Kuang's prose here is expertly sculpted. In fact, Katabasis represents an improvement over her prose style in Babel. She seems to have completely mastered organically allowing the sentence structure to imitate the tone and thus influence the reader's mood. Her diction, never high handed and never purple, is exquisitely measured and timed. One of my favorite opening sections details why our protagonist intends to make a quite dangerous rescue attempt. She introduces the local color of her magic system with several eclectic names for laws and principles, makes several subtly humorous allusions to Dante and TS Elliott. And then, at the end of the paragraph, Kuang adds bluntly and plainly, "this meant she had to go to hell, and she had to go today." It's the first time such simple diction has been used in this paragraph, and it's just the perfect touch. Another example of Kuang's evolved use of organic structure occurs when she uses repetition to emote exactly the opposite of what is said. To clarify, when Kuang offers exposition on Alice's conflict, she repeats the opening phrase of descriptive sentences. "No one saw" Alice's involvement in her professor's death, and "no one saw" when she took off her bloody clothes, and "no one saw" her incinerate them. The phrase "no one saw" is repeated multiple times throughout the paragraph. It's repeated so often that readers begin to doubt the validity of the phrase. They begin to understand that Alice is worried about just the opposite--that someone must have seen. Each time the phrase is repeated, the anxiety builds. Readers sincerely feel Alice's fear, her need to reassure herself, and the emotion is so much stronger because it is indirectly conveyed by structure instead of being merely stated. Kuang's prose depicts exactly the artistry possible when authors wield a mastery of grammar and form. There's nothing pedantic or even didactic in the descriptions. They are just as carefully crafted as the sentence structure. Ignore the one star reviews that say otherwise. This is the sort of novel that belongs on Locus Award nomination lists. Katabasis is literature. Further Thoughts:Kuang's books have always offered readers a carefully laid out symbology. But Katabasis is next level. Her motifs weave thematic layers upon layers so perfectly that the ending tapestry is a marvel. Bluntly, I can't even understand how brilliant one would have to be to pull off something like this. It's that sort of book--the sort you want to spend months deconstructing just so you can polish your own awe into language. I had not originally intended to write a review for this one, mainly because it's already so widely reviewed, but I have seen such a baffling smear campaign against what is essentially Kuang's most impressive work to date, that I felt compelled after a second reading to throw in my two cents. I really urge fantasy lovers, especially academic fantasy lovers, not to miss out. Of all the hardcovers on which I have spent far too much coin, this is one first edition I will never regret. This book is beautiful beyond its colorful jacket and sprayed edges. Bottomline: If you are a reader of literature, then you will love this book. If you are used to the average national bestseller, no problem--you can still love this book. Just go into it with a search engine at the ready and take your time. It's worth it.
