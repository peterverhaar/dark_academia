7917837755	I don't expect this review to have any influence, there are over 500 reviews against this edition on GR and as the stories in this title have appeared again and again under various titles, never mind excerpted individually into other anthologies, the actual number of reviews is almost impossible to calculate. But in terms of 'horror' fiction James is review proof, rather like H.P. Lovecraft, the other writer of 'classic/vintage' horror to whom he is often compared (but more about Lovecraft later).I first read James when I was at school in Ireland, I cannot remember the edition it certainly wasn't this one, and I also know it was not an edition of the complete stories. Who knows but maybe I actually read a first edition? Such things were not unlikely in the 1970s. The stories impressed me but, I cannot separate my response to the stories on the page from the BBC adaptations of five of his stories for their 'Ghost story or Christmas' series - one in particular 'Lost Hearts' was to haunt me for years long after I had forgotten its origin on TV or as a James story. All of this is really a prelude to saying that I can't really step back and judge these stories independently because they are to inextricably tied up whether as original stories or adaptations in my young formative adolescent years. I have read them again with pleasure and if I had now read them for the first time I am sure I would still have enjoyed them. But would they have the same impact? Probably not but all the same I award the stories the stars and shelving accolades that I couldn't award them as a 12/13 year old.I think one of the reasons I enjoyed James's stories was that as a child in the 1960s and an adolescent in the 1970s my 'horror' sensibility was formed in part by classic Hollywood horror but even more by the Hammer horror films. The atmosphere of the Hammer films created out of the detritus of Victorian/Edwardian set dressing and costumes easily dovetailed or was adapted in my imagination to James's stories. Frock coats, gentlemen scholars/archaeologists, ancient incunabula it is surprising how many of these tropes were to be found even as late as Buffy the Vampire Slayer TV series (1997-2001).What I can do now is contrast the stories by James with the work of H.P. Lovecraft (which I didn't read until I was much, much older). I don't like Lovecraft and find that his barely concealed racism towards non WASP immigrants and not-at-all concealed obsession with miscegenation and his desire to see the state step in and put a stop to it I find utterly repellent. As far as I am concerned the ugly basis of so much of Lovecraft's fiction is not excused by the, to me, banalities of his Cthulu Mythos which I recognise is, inexplicably, a source of inspiration to writers today.But of course when I compare Lovecraft with James I can't help noting that the later wrote most of his best stories in the years before WWI - in that retrospectively glorious, if morally flawed, era of late Victorian/Edwardian grandeur when Britain comfortably ruled the waves and so much of the world and to be an English gentleman was the unquestioned, by other English gentlemen, cynosure of everyone's desire. Lovecraft on the other hand wrote most of his stories post WWI, and he seems to have adopted, and clung to, the fashionable ideas of Oswald Spengler that Scott Fitzgerald so satirised in 'The Great Gatsby' via the character of the oafish Tom Buchanann who misidentifies Spengler's name and that of his famous book, which makes his earnest and pessimistic rant about racial supremacy and the decay civilisation even more absurd.James is not a mouthpiece for British imperialism, though no doubt he was one, he emerges from his time but is not bound by it, rather like those 'Victorian' settings for the Hammer horror films. Lovecraft seems, to me, much more of his time and to echo, loudly, its ugliest features.But as I admit James was a formative force of my earliest most impressionable adolescent years. The only other author from those years I still cherish is Saki who is infinitely greater than James. So I can't step back and judge him - I leave you to do that for yourselves.
